## Part IV: The Graviton Question

### Have We Detected Gravitons?

**No.** Not once. Not ever.

We detected gravitational **waves** (LIGO, 2015). These are classical ripples in spacetime curvature. Detecting them no more proves gravitons exist than detecting ocean waves proves water is made of discrete "water particles."

A graviton would be a **quantum** of the gravitational field ‚Äî a single particle mediating gravitational interaction, analogous to how photons mediate electromagnetism.

### Why Haven't We Detected Them?

Gravity is extraordinarily weak. A detector capable of registering a single graviton would need to be **the size of Jupiter** (Dyson, 2012). This isn't a technology problem we'll solve with better instruments. It's a fundamental signal-to-noise issue.

Freeman Dyson argued we may never detect individual gravitons **even in principle**.

### What Does This Mean for CCF Theory?

The absence of graviton detection is **not** evidence for CCF theory. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

But it does mean the *assumption* that gravity must be quantized like other forces is **untested**. It's theoretical extrapolation, not experimental fact.

CCF theory proposes gravity doesn't need gravitons because gravity isn't a force transmitted between objects. Gravity is the **shape of the substrate** ‚Äî geometry emerging from coherence patterns.

If the Coherence Telephone works, it supports this view: topology couples to something pre-geometric. If that something gives rise to spacetime, then gravity is a downstream consequence, not a fundamental interaction requiring its own force carriers.
